## Design Context

### Users
Business operations managers and people analytics leads building strategic workforce plans, presenting findings to group leaders and directors. Users are not necessarily data-literate in a technical sense — they are narrative-first thinkers who need the tool to make scenarios legible and credible to senior audiences. The interface must work both as a live browser demo in a meeting room and as a source of screenshots for slide packs.

### Brand Personality
Exploratory, Accurate, Professional.

The interface should invite curiosity and scenario-testing without feeling like a playground. It should communicate rigour and trustworthiness without feeling cold or clinical. The tone is akin to a well-prepared management briefing: confident, clear, evidence-grounded, never alarmist or decorative for decoration's sake.

### Corporate Palette
| Role | Name | Hex |
|------|------|-----|
| Prime | Black | `#1A1A1A` |
| Prime | White | `#FFFFFF` |
| Secondary | Bright Orange | `#FF5C32` |
| Secondary | Light Orange | `#FFA851` |
| Secondary | Blue | `#444AFF` |
| Tertiary | Purple | `#9BA0F9` |
| Tertiary | Grey | `#75808B` |

Orange is the warmth signal — use it to soften what would otherwise be a stark black-and-white palette. Blue is the precision/action signal (interactive elements, primary data series). Purple is a supporting accent only. Grey is for supporting text, borders, and disabled states.

### Theme
Light and dark mode, switchable. Default to light — this tool is most often used in daytime presentation contexts (meeting rooms, desk work). Dark mode for low-light or personal preference.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Not clinical**: Avoid pure cool-grey palettes and hospital-report aesthetics. Orange accents and generous whitespace keep it human.
- **Not extreme**: No brutalism, no maximalist decoration, no playful illustration. The audience is executives; the output is a strategic document.
- **Presentation-ready**: Every tab and chart should be screenshot-worthy. Avoid layout elements that look unfinished or prototype-like in isolation.
- **Anti-references**: Generic SaaS dashboard templates, dark-mode data tools with cyan/purple neon accents, anything that looks like a developer built it for developers.

### Typography
- **Display/headings**: Bricolage Grotesque — variable, professionally distinctive, warm without being playful. Works well for scenario labels and KPI values.
- **Body/labels/data**: Manrope — legible at small sizes, slightly warm, pairs well with Bricolage without competing.
- Fixed `rem` scale for UI elements (this is a product UI, not a marketing page). Fluid only if a specific view calls for large headline numbers.

### Design Principles
1. **Legibility is the primary aesthetic act.** Every design decision — spacing, colour, type size — should first serve the reader's ability to extract insight quickly.
2. **Orange earns its place.** Use bright orange for risk signals, tipping points, and alerts. Use light orange for supporting warmth. Never use orange decoratively.
3. **Slide-safe at all times.** Any chart or data panel should be screenshot-ready. Avoid decorative elements that look incomplete in isolation.
4. **Restrained interactivity.** Interactions (toggles, filters, sliders) should feel precise and considered — not playful. Each control should have a clear effect the user can explain to a director.
5. **Space is credibility.** Generous whitespace around data signals confidence and careful preparation. Crowded layouts signal prototype-thinking.
